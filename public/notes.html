Speaker Notes: Project Canopy

01 — Introduction

[Show leopard image]

Thank you for being here. Today I want to talk about a species on the brink: the clouded leopard. When I first started researching this project, I expected to find a well-studied animal. Instead, I found one of the most elusive cats on Earth, with fewer than 10,000 left in the wild and nearly a third of its habitat lost in 20 years. That number stuck with me: 30 percent gone in a single generation. Clouded leopards are extraordinary. Their ankles rotate 180 degrees, they descend trees headfirst, and they have the longest canine teeth relative to body size of any living cat. They're built for the canopy in ways no other predator is. But they can't survive without it, and the canopy is disappearing.

[Pause. Let this sink in.]

02 — Geographic Range

[Show range map]

So where do these animals live? The clouded leopard's range stretches from the eastern Himalayas (Nepal, Bhutan, northeast India) through mainland Southeast Asia to southern China. That sounds large, and historically it was. Today those populations are fragmented into isolated forest blocks. Logging, roads, and agriculture have carved up what used to be continuous habitat. One important note: the Sunda clouded leopard on Borneo and Sumatra is actually a separate species, Neofelis diardi, recognized in 2006. Our plan focuses on the mainland species, Neofelis nebulosa, across these 11 range countries highlighted here.

03 — Goals & Objectives

[Show the five objectives]

Our overarching goal is to grow the wild population to 15,000 individuals across connected habitat within 25 years. That means: protect 50,000 square kilometers of forest, cut poaching mortality by 90 percent, maintain 120+ breeding individuals in captive care, build community economies around conservation, and advance research through camera traps and GPS collars. But why does this species matter beyond the numbers? Ecologically, clouded leopards regulate prey populations and prevent overgrazing. Their loss triggers trophic cascades that destabilize food webs. Scientifically, they are the oldest lineage in Pantherinae, diverged up to nine million years ago. They cannot roar or purr, sitting in an evolutionary gap that teaches us more about felid evolution than almost any living cat. Culturally, communities across Southeast Asia have deep ties to this animal, making them natural conservation allies.

04 — Solutions

[Show protection map]

So what are we doing? The plan has four pillars. First, habitat protection and restoration: securing primary forest, funding ranger patrols, and rebuilding canopy corridors at least two kilometers wide between isolated reserves. These corridors must be tall, continuous canopy, not ground-level plantings, because clouded leopards are arboreal. Second, anti-poaching through SMART patrols, intelligence networks, and community informant programs. Third, captive breeding. The SSP currently manages about 70 clouded leopards in North American zoos. We need to expand that and solve male aggression by pairing animals young. Fourth, community engagement. Conservation fails if local people don't benefit. We're building ecotourism and livelihood programs, showing communities that a living leopard is worth more than a dead one.

05 — Timeline

[Walk through the three phases]

The 25-year plan has three phases. Phase I (2027–2032) is foundation: completing the range-wide population survey, establishing ten new protected areas covering 8,000 square kilometers, deploying SMART patrols, and launching community programs in 50 villages. Phase II (2033–2040) is active recovery: finishing corridor plantings between five major reserves, reducing snare density by 80 percent, running pilot reintroductions, and scaling ecotourism to 100,000 visitors per year. Phase III (2041–2051) aims to hit 15,000 wild individuals, confirm genetic connectivity across habitat blocks, transition patrol funding to national governments, and petition the IUCN to reclassify the species from Vulnerable to Near Threatened. Each phase builds on the last: you can't reintroduce animals into unrestored habitat.

06 — Funding

[Show funding breakdown]

The full 25-year plan costs approximately 260 million dollars. That sounds large, so let me put it in perspective. The forests these leopards live in provide an estimated 7.5 billion dollars in ecosystem services annually: carbon storage, flood prevention, clean drinking water, and non-timber products. Research in Nature values Southeast Asian tropical forests at roughly $1,500 per hectare per year. For every dollar we invest in recovery, we preserve 28 dollars in ecosystem value. The biggest line items are protected-area establishment (80 million), ranger programs (55 million), and reforestation (40 million). This isn't charity. It's an investment with a measurable return.

07 — Measuring Success

[Show success metrics]

A plan is only as good as its accountability. We measure success through six core metrics on set schedules. Camera-trap surveys every two years give population density and individual identification via coat-pattern recognition. Genetic monitoring every three years tracks inbreeding and gene flow between fragments using non-invasive fecal DNA. Satellite imagery is analyzed annually using Global Forest Watch and LiDAR to quantify forest cover and corridor connectivity. Anti-poaching metrics (snares removed, patrol kilometers, arrests) are reported quarterly. We track captive population health through the SSP studbook and survey community income and attitudes every two years. All metrics are reported to stakeholders annually, ensuring full transparency on what's working and what needs to change.

A brief note on my research process. I relied on peer-reviewed literature and established conservation databases. The IUCN Red List assessment by Grassman, Lynam, and Mohamad was my primary source for population status and threat data. It's the gold standard for species conservation. For deforestation numbers, I used Global Forest Watch and the Curtis et al. 2018 Science paper on drivers of global forest loss. The Smithsonian's National Zoo provided detailed information on captive breeding challenges and the SSP. I also drew on Panthera's field research, TRAFFIC's illegal wildlife trade reports, and the Clouded Leopard Project's work. Every claim is sourced, and the full citations are on the site.
