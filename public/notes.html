Speaker Notes: Project Canopy

Opening

[Show leopard image]

Thank you for being here. Today I want to talk about a species on the brink. The clouded leopard is one of the world's most elusive big cats, and we're losing them fast. In just 20 years, they've lost nearly a third of their habitat. We have fewer than 10,000 left in the wild. But we have a plan to save them.

[Pause. Let this sink in.]

Why They Matter

[Reference leopard abilities]

These are incredible animals. Their ankles rotate backward. They hang upside down from branches. They're built for the forest canopy in ways almost no other predator is. When we save the clouded leopard, we're really saving the entire Southeast Asian rainforest ecosystem they depend on. That means protecting millions of acres of carbon-storing forest, clean water, and the homes of countless other species.

The Problem

[Show deforestation statistics]

Deforestation is the biggest threat. Logging, palm oil plantations, rubber farming. These are tearing apart the canopy that clouded leopards need to survive. On top of that, there's poaching for illegal wildlife markets. Roads and human settlements carve up the forest, isolating populations unable to breed with each other. Climate change is making things worse by shrinking the mountain forests where they take refuge.

Our Solution

[Show protection map]

We're protecting what's left and restoring what's been lost. We're building canopy bridges to reconnect isolated populations. We're funding ranger patrols to stop poaching. We're maintaining a genetic backup through breeding programs in zoos. And we're working with local communities to show them that leopard conservation benefits everyone, not just wildlife.

The Investment

[Show funding breakdown]

The full 25-year plan costs about 260 million dollars. That sounds like a lot, but the forests these leopards live in provide 7.5 billion dollars in ecosystem services every year. Carbon storage, flood prevention, clean drinking water. For every dollar we invest in this recovery, we get back 28 dollars in ecosystem value. This is good economics and good conservation.

Closing

[Show compelling final image]

In 25 years, we want to see 15,000 clouded leopards thriving in the wild. We want reconnected forests where they can roam and breed freely. We want local communities thriving because of leopard conservation, not despite it. Saving the clouded leopard is about more than one species. It's about protecting the canopy for all life.

[Pause. Let silence sit.]
